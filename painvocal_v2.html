How acoustic pain detection works

Click each sound type to see its frequency profile

Healthy flock — low frequency, rhythmic
A healthy flock produces a constant low murmur — contact calls, feeding sounds, and gentle clucking. The acoustic profile is dominated by low frequencies (200–800 Hz) and is rhythmic and predictable.
Why vocalisation reveals pain
Chickens have over 30 distinct call types. When experiencing pain, birds produce higher-pitched calls with sharper onset and faster call rate. In BCO lameness specifically, distress calls emerge 2–3 days before visible gait problems — giving farmers an intervention window that visual inspection completely misses.
The classifier pipeline
Raw audio → noise filtering → mel-spectrogram (converts sound to an image of frequencies over time) → CNN image classifier → flock-level aggregator → alert. The spectrogram step is key: it turns a hard audio problem into an image classification problem that modern AI handles well.
The bottleneck (and why Myrias can fix it)
The science is established. Published studies show >85% classification accuracy in controlled settings. The gap is a labeled training dataset from real commercial barns — requiring producer partnerships and veterinary confirmation. This is exactly the "valley of death" between proof-of-concept and deployment that the Myrias/Innovate Animal Ag model is designed to bridge.

PainVocal as a Myrias project

Click each stage to expand — modelled on the Innovate Animal Ag DARPA approach

1
Research validation — 0 to 6 months
Partner with a poultry science lab (University of Arkansas already works with Innovate Animal Ag) to collect labeled audio from BCO lameness, respiratory disease, and healthy flocks. Fine-tune a lightweight mel-spectrogram CNN classifier. Establish baseline accuracy benchmarks. Budget: ~$80–120K — comparable to typical Innovate Animal Ag research grants.
›
2
Commercial trial — 6 to 18 months
Deploy prototype sensor arrays in 3–5 barns with an early-adopter producer (NestFresh-style partner — already demonstrated appetite for welfare innovation with in-ovo sexing). Measure alert-to-intervention lag, false positive rate, and correlation with mortality outcomes. Build the ROI case: if detecting BCO early prevents even 10% more pre-slaughter deaths, the system pays for itself.
›
3
Certification integration — 12 to 24 months
Build welfare data log export for third-party audit bodies. Pitch to Better Chicken Commitment signatories as low-cost compliance infrastructure — giving retailers a credible welfare story without the cost of pastoral housing. This cracks the collective action logjam: companies that were rationalising non-compliance suddenly have an affordable alternative.
›
4
Scale and spin-out — 24 months plus
License the classifier to existing barn tech companies, or spin out as a standalone Myrias portfolio company (for-profit or nonprofit depending on what the problem needs). Expand species coverage to pigs and turkeys — same core technology, massive additional scale. Pork industry pre-slaughter mortality is ~10% with almost no acoustic monitoring in place.
›
For-profit path
SaaS subscription per barn. Revenue sustains the company and scales distribution. Farmers pay because the ROI is real — fewer dead birds means recovered revenue.
Nonprofit path
Open-source classifier plus grant-funded deployment for maximum coverage speed. Pairs with philanthropic capital from foundations already funding animal welfare tech.
Hybrid (most likely fit for Myrias)
Open-source the classifier — lowering the barrier to adoption industry-wide. Commercial dashboard and support layer generates revenue. Mirrors how Innovate Animal Ag operates: nonprofit mission, pragmatic about for-profit tools where they accelerate impact.
